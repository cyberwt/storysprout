## Project description

### What I built

StorySprout is a creative learning experience that helps children practice narrative thinking and creative confidence through a gentle bedtime story. Instead of passively watching a story, a child becomes the author of its important moments.

For this demo, children join Poppy the dog in **Poppy and the Lost Star**. They add their name, choose a visual story world, and guide Poppy through three meaningful choices: what to pack, how to help a shy firefly, and the promise Poppy makes at the end. Those choices become part of the completed storybook, which can be narrated in the browser and extended into an offline imaginative-play prompt.

### Why it matters

Many children rehearse stories while playing alone with toys. StorySprout is designed as a warm co-play space that meets that impulse with curiosity and structure: it helps a child put ideas into sequence, make decisions, and see that their choices change a story. It is not presented as a parent, caregiver, or sibling replacement; the child remains in charge.

### How it works

1. The child enters their name and the name of their story companion.
2. They choose a story-world treatment, such as cozy watercolor or starlight fable.
3. They move through three illustrated scenes and make one choice per scene.
4. The final book turns those choices into a natural, personalized bedtime ending.
5. A real-world play prompt helps the story continue away from the screen.

### How I built it

StorySprout is a React and Vite web app with an original CSS illustration system, responsive interface, scene animation, and browser-native narration through the Web Speech API. The demo is deliberately a fixed, polished story rather than an unconstrained runtime generator: that keeps the experience reliable, age-appropriate, and easy for judges to run without credentials.

I built it collaboratively with Codex: from the early product direction and user flow, through the React implementation, narrative choice structure, interface refinement, original stargazer-and-dog hero illustration, and final production-build verification. See the README for setup instructions and a fuller implementation note.

### Challenges I ran into

The hardest product decision was balancing the feeling of an imaginative companion with clear boundaries. I avoided framing the product as a replacement for a caregiver, and made the child—not the narrator—the source of every important decision. I also chose a deterministic story for the hackathon demo so its emotional tone, pacing, and ending remain dependable.

### Accomplishments I’m proud of

- Turning a child’s three small choices into a cohesive finished story.
- Creating an original, warm storybook visual system without relying on third-party character art.
- Making a complete flow that runs locally with no API key or account.
- Ending the screen experience with a tangible, real-world imaginative-play invitation.

### What I learned

An AI-enabled children’s product needs more than a clever prompt. The key design questions are authorship, emotional framing, and predictability. The strongest version of StorySprout treats technology as a gentle creative tool while protecting the child’s agency.

### What’s next for StorySprout

The next version will add parent-approved story prompts, optional saved family storybooks, accessible pre-rendered narration, and carefully bounded AI-assisted story expansion. The goal is to make each child’s ideas feel seen while keeping the experience warm, useful, and safe.
